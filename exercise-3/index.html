Exercise 3: Design Principles

Group A: Contrast

Follows the Principle: Apple Homepage

https://www.apple.com

The Apple homepage uses large, bold headlines on a clean white or very dark background plus high-quality product photos, which creates strong contrast and makes the main message and call-to-action stand out clearly. This kind of strong but controlled contrast improves clarity and legibility.

Violates the Principle: Ling’s Cars

https://www.lingscars.com

Ling’s Cars uses extremely saturated backgrounds, flashing graphics, and many clashing colors, so nothing stands out and the content competes with the visuals instead of being highlighted.

How I’d Improve It:

I’d cut the color palette down to 2–3 main colors, tone down the neon backgrounds, and use dark text on light sections (or vice versa) so the headings, prices, and buttons are easy to see at a glance.

Group B: Proximity

Follows the Principle: Dropbox Homepage

https://www.dropbox.com

The Dropbox homepage groups its main headline, short description, and primary “Sign up” button together in one central block, with other options and links placed in clearly separated sections, so related elements are physically close and easy to scan.

Violates the Principle: Yale School of Art

https://www.art.yale.edu

The Yale School of Art site has text boxes, images, and links scattered around with little spacing or grouping, creating a jumbled layout where related information is not clearly clustered.

How I’d Improve It:

I’d reorganize the page into clear sections (e.g., navigation at the top, news in one column, events in another) and add consistent spacing so that items that belong together are visually grouped and unrelated content is separated.
